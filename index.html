About the Workshop

Automotive radar is uniquely resilient to adverse weather and low-light conditions, yet it remains a largely underexplored modality in generative simulation. Collecting and annotating real-world radar data is costly to scale, making high-fidelity radar simulation a key bottleneck for the safe deployment of autonomous vehicles. AuRAS brings together researchers from academia and industry across radar signal processing, generative modeling, and autonomous driving to build a shared forum for this emerging challenge. Through invited talks, a poster session, and a panel discussion, we explore physics-based and data-driven radar simulation, the generation of realistic radar data including point clouds and raw signals, and how radar simulation enables and advances downstream perception tasks and real-world applications.

Topics of Interest (not limited to)

  • Generative and physics-based automotive radar simulation
  • Raw radar signal modeling and synthesis
  • Automotive radar novel view synthesis and neural scene representations
  • Datasets, benchmarks, and metrics for evaluation of radar simulation methods
  • Radar simulation for perception training, evaluation, and real-world deployment

Submission Guidelines

  • Tracks
    • Short papers: We invite short papers of up to 4 pages. Unlimited references and appendix.
    • Accepted papers: We welcome papers already accepted at other venues. These are not eligible for oral presentation and are limited to at most half of the accepted papers.
  • Template Use the official NeurIPS 2026 LaTeX template. Submissions must be in PDF format.
  • Presentation All accepted papers are presented as posters following the main-conference guidelines; 2 short papers are selected for oral presentations.
  • Archival Policy Non-archival: accepted papers are not part of the official NeurIPS proceedings, so you retain the right to submit elsewhere.
  • Review Process Short papers undergo a double-blind review and must therefore be fully anonymized; previously accepted papers are exempt from anonymization.
